# Cover Letter Template

Canonical letter text. Twin static PDF (human-edited draft): `documents/cover-letter-template.pdf`.

**Runtime rule:** never upload the static template. The fill engine always:
1. Fills last-paragraph placeholders from the Notion JD (`[COMPANY]`, mission/value, two skills)
2. Writes a tailored PDF under `data/fill/cover-letters/{Name}-{Company}-cover-letter.pdf`
3. Uploads that generated file (or pastes the same text into a cover-letter textarea)

Placeholders in the **last paragraph only** are filled per job from the Notion job description:

- `[COMPANY]` → company name
- `[REFER TO MISSION STATEMENT AND/OR CORE VALUE HERE]` → mission / values / product purpose from the JD (never the role title, never a queue JD scrap). If none is found, a short generic fit phrase.
- `[RELEVANT SKILL 1]` / `[RELEVANT SKILL 2]` → skills from the JD that match the skills profile

Most software engineers are taught to write code and tackle well-defined problems. Far fewer learn to question whether the problem itself is the right one to solve. Rarer still are those who are comfortable navigating ambiguity and forging clarity where none exists.

I chose to study both Computer Science and Philosophy to understand not just how to create, but also what is worth creating, and why. This deliberate path shaped my ability to grapple with what I believe is the hardest challenge in software development today. The real test is not simply writing code, but having the judgment to determine what truly needs to be built.

At my startup, my team and I faced an early choice: whether to build something familiar and safe, or to pursue what the market actually needed, even if it meant months of uncertainty before a single line of code was written. We chose the harder path. For weeks, we immersed ourselves in customer discovery, listening to potential users and learning an industry that was entirely new to us. When we didn't know something, we found people who did. That process taught me that the willingness to stay in the uncomfortable space of not knowing, and to keep moving anyway, is what separates work that matters from work that merely gets finished.

What surprised me most about my most ambitious project was how often the hardest problems had nothing to do with code. While owning the entire AI backend myself, I was simultaneously coordinating a UI designer and a frontend developer whose work was tightly interdependent, even though neither was my direct responsibility. Making sure each of them got what they needed from the other, while simultaneously preventing the project from going off the rails through miscommunication and ensuring both people were moving in the same direction, required a different kind of thinking entirely. That experience made concrete what my Philosophy background had only ever taught me in the abstract. The hardest part of any project is rarely the coding. It is knowing what needs to happen, when, and for whom. Knowing when to put my head down and build, and when to step back and lead, is a skill I now carry into every project I take on.
